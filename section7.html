AI Policy

Why and how AI is used in this course, and your disclosure duties.

Why Use AI In the Course

It is important to understand and critically analyze how AI operates across contexts, because these operations implicate how such programs are designed, how individuals access social and economic participation, education, and how they are perceived. Normalizing power epistemologies through the function of AI algorithms creates “otherness” that can have detrimental consequences, as seen in some of the case studies we will read in class. Moreover, algorithms often treat gender and other identity markers one at a time, rather than the intersectional aspects of lived experiences. Feminist and intersectionality theory gives the tools to analyze power structures that leave groups and experiences out of the AI design table—asking not just whether these systems are biased, but toward whom. Critical making asks us to test such critiques by embodying them through building outside of the digital space; the object itself is the argument.

This course is about generative AI, which means working with it, not just reading about it. You will use AI tools regularly throughout the semester, track your interactions in a prompt journal, and build your final project from that record. You do not need to trust AI, like it, or believe it is good for the world. The course is not about leaving makers of such technologies outside but reshaping our thinking as users and designers. Therefore, the focus is that through direct engagement, you can enhance a critical stance on how they represent and reconstruct identities. Part of taking a critical stance is asking whose labor and values are embedded in the data based on the outputs you have received from your inputted prompt.

AI & The Course

AI is the core critical aspect of the course, not an optional supplement to it. Every student generates their own AI interactions, logs them in their journal, and builds a final artifact (either through AI or physical) from the journaling. The case studies and readings (see section 4 and section 5) will model the critical analysis you will apply in your prompts connected to the 3 identity markers of your choice.

AI & Journaling

Your journal is treated as a record and a source, which you should reference in MLA citation when using it in any of the assignments in class. You can record your journal entries in the following forms:

  • screenshots of the inputted prompts of the AI outputs with a short reflection;
  • written prompt inputs, screenshot of the outputs with a short reflection.

If you are using an AI-generated text or image artifact in any of the course projects, disclose it in a statement: how you used it, and why you used it. Remember that AI algorithms put information together from millions of other harvested data; while the critique is your work, the generated text/visual is not yours.

AI & Uses

In this course we will generate:

  • text responses;
  • distant reading analysis;
  • final creative design artifacts.

Disclose each generative program you used, your intention for use, and why you chose the program in connection to your assignment’s argument.

AI & Other Tools

For this course, you are allowed to use the free versions of Claude, ChatGPT, and any other tool we use. Nevertheless, keep in mind that you might get more creative and your projects more robust with a paid subscription, which will provide longer use and better results—but no assignment will require so much unless you choose to. Should you have any issues accessing the generative tools, please see the professor for university resources available.
