Course Description

What the course is about and the critical lens it takes on AI.

This course is about how artificial intelligence shapes how we are and how we interact with our environment. “Intersectional Machines” addresses two theories: feminist and intersectional. This brings our experiences to the table to address how AI shapes and reconstructs identity. In academia and industry, AI is being implemented without our input or consent, embodied as a necessity and the threshold to obtain access to software and jobs requiring AI as a skill set. Hence, AI is shaping the knowledge and access to spaces.

Nevertheless, this is either or widening the historical gender gap in some of these spaces, which reflects the historically inherent social biases reflected in harvested data in generative AI technologies. Therefore, this course is built through reading, writing, and making as three intersecting practices that will drive into directly engaging with AI from a critical lens. The critical nature of this course is not to persuade you to either start or stop using the technology, but to understand its affordances and limitations.

Three intersecting practices drive the course: reading, writing, and making. Together they build a critical stance on how AI represents and reconstructs identity.

Course units

The semester is organized into four units, each about four weeks.

UNIT 1 (objective 1&2)

Foundations in feminist and intersectionality theory (4 weeks)

  • Foundations in feminist theory and intersectionality
  • Foundations in how generative artificial intelligence works, falling outside of marketed neutrality
  • Low-Stakes Assignment Using your prompts: Claude Artifact Generating A Textual Map (5% at 10pts)
  • Project 1: On-going towards end of the semester (Submit part 1 of journal entries 10% of course grade)
  • Keep a semester long track of student prompts they will create and input into GAI and track its outputs over the course of the semester to create a textual rhetorical mapping of algorithmic fragmentation and recomposing of identity
UNIT 2 (objective 4)

Writing/Visualizing with & Against GAI (4 weeks)

  • GAI prompt writing as rhetorical practice
  • Analyzing output code
  • Project 1: Submit part 2 of journal entries (15% of course grade)
  • Examine what happens to their language, dialect, cultural registers when processed by GAI tools introduced in unit 1.
UNIT 3 (objective 1-4)

Making as Inquiry, Analysis, & Critique (4 weeks)

  • Analyze AI-generated text and images for what gets flattened, reconstructed, or erased, recording their prompt inputs in their journals to track these changes.
  • Project 2: Begin working on the Artifact (45% of course grade submitted end-of-semester)
  • Project 1: Submit part 3 of journal entries (15% of course grade)
UNIT 4 (objective 3)

Creating A Response (4 weeks)

  • Students will create a final artifact (zine, website, visual) that responds to their findings. All AI use is cited and disclosed.
  • Project 3: Written Response submitted at end-of-semester (10% of course grade)
  • Submission of the artifact to be submitted
